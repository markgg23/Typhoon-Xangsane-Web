The Aftermath
Following the passage of Xangsane, the International Federation of Red Cross and Red Crescent Societies (IFRC) issued an emergency request for 5.7 million Swiss francs ($4.6 million, 2006 USD) to aid the Philippine Red Cross in its relief effort. In addition, the movement dispatched 3,000 workers and three disaster response teams to affected areas for search and rescue missions, damage assessments, and assistance to people affected by the typhoon.
Typhoon Xangsane
September 25, 2006, typhoon Xangsane developed in the western Pacific, close to the Philippine Islands' coast. In the following thirty-six hours, it developed into a typhoon from a tropical depression, an area of low air pressure.
The Effects
Typhoon Xangsane (local name Milenyo), which landed on the Philippines on 28 September 2006, devastated the central part of the country, including the capital Manila, and affected 20 provinces. On 10 October, the National Disaster Coordinating Council reported that 153 people were killed, 406 people were injured and 75 people were missing.
The Wind Force
On September 27, PAGASA issued Storm Signal Number 3, a warning for winds of 100–185 km/h (62–115 mph), for areas of southern Luzon. Storm Signal Number 2, for winds of 60–100 km/h (37–62 mph), was raised for northern Samar Island and central Luzon. Storm Signal Number 1 (30–60 km/h, 20–37 mph) was issued for northwestern Luzon, including Metro Manila, and northern Visayas, including the rest of Samar Island. Schools in the warned area were suspended, and airports and seaports were closed.
The Damages
The Philippines saw extensive damage, especially in Metro Manila, which the storm nearly skirted over. Numerous provinces had power and water outages, and highways became inaccessible due to flooding and landslides caused by overflowing rivers. There were up to six days without power in certain areas of the nation.>
